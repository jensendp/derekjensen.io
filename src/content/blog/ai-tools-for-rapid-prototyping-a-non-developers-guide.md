## How to Go From Idea to Working Prototype in One Sitting

Here's the part most people overcomplicate. You don't need a business plan or wireframes. You need one clear sentence about what you want to build.

Let's walk through a real example using Lovable.

**Step 1: Describe what you want in plain English.** Open Lovable and type something like:

```
Build a landing page for a dog walking service called PawPals.
Include a headline, three benefits, customer testimonials,
and a signup form that collects name, email, and zip code.
Use a friendly, modern design with a blue and white color scheme.
```

**Step 2: Let the tool generate your first version.** Within about 30 seconds, you'll have a working page. It won't be perfect. That's fine.

**Step 3: Refine with follow-up prompts.** Say things like "Make the color scheme blue and white" or "Add a pricing section with three tiers." Each tweak takes seconds. Here's an example of a solid follow-up prompt:

```
Add a pricing section below the testimonials with three tiers:
- "Casual" at $15/walk (1-2 walks per week)
- "Regular" at $12/walk (3-4 walks per week)
- "Daily" at $10/walk (5+ walks per week)
Highlight the "Regular" tier as the most popular option.
```

> **Tip:** Write your prompts like you're briefing a designer — be specific about layout, content, and structure. "Add a pricing section" is okay. "Add a pricing section with three tiers, prices, and a highlighted best-value option" is much better. For more on getting the most from your prompts, read the [prompt engineering guide for builders](https://derekjensen.io/blog/prompt-engineering-for-builders-the-complete-guide).

**Step 4: Click through it yourself.** Test the buttons. Fill out the form. See how it feels on mobile.

The whole process takes 15–30 minutes. That's AI tools for rapid prototyping in action.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Writing prompts that are too vague ("make me a website" gives you generic junk)
- Trying to build everything at once instead of starting simple and iterating
- Skipping the mobile preview — half your users will see it on their phone first

Start small. Get something real on screen. Then make it better. If you want to go deeper on what trips people up, the guide on [beginner mistakes using AI to code](https://derekjensen.io/blog/beginner-mistakes-using-ai-to-code-and-how-to-fix-them) covers the most common pitfalls.

## What Makes a Good Prototype (and What These Tools Can't Do Yet)

Before you start building, it helps to know what you're actually making. These three terms get mixed up all the time:

- **A prototype** shows how something looks and feels. It's clickable. People can interact with it. But it's not built to handle real users long-term.
- **An MVP** (minimum viable product) is a stripped-down version that real people actually use. It needs to work reliably.
- **A production app** is the full, polished thing — fast, secure, and ready to scale.

AI tools for rapid prototyping are incredible at that first step. They get you to a working prototype faster than anything else available in 2026. But they're not magic. If you're curious about what's realistic and what isn't, check out [what AI can and cannot build today](https://derekjensen.io/blog/what-ai-can-and-cannot-build-today-guide).

Here's where they still struggle:

- **Complex logic.** If your app needs lots of rules that depend on each other, the AI will make mistakes. Things like multi-step payment flows or permission systems can get messy fast.
- **Database design.** AI can set up a basic database, but it doesn't always organize data the way a real app needs long-term.
- **Security and scale.** A prototype doesn't need to handle 10,000 users. A production app does. That gap still matters.

Don't let this discourage you. Set your goal clearly: build something people can see, click, and react to. That's a prototype's job. If the response is good, then you figure out the next step.

A good prototype gets you answers. It doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to be real enough to learn from.
