## Why You Need Monthly AI Stack Recommendations (Not a Static List)

Here's the thing about AI tools — they change fast. Like, really fast.

A tool that was the clear winner in March might get outpaced by April. Prices shift. Features get added or removed. New tools pop up that make old ones pointless.

That's why a static "best tools" list stops being useful almost the moment someone publishes it. What you actually need is a regular check-in. A monthly habit where you look at what you're paying for, what you're actually using, and what's changed in the landscape.

I call this a living stack. Instead of picking your tools once and forgetting about them, you treat your stack like something that grows and shrinks with you.

The alternative? Tool bloat. That's when you're subscribed to six things but only opening two. The average non-developer I talk to wastes $50 to $80 a month on redundant subscriptions. ## How I Evaluate Tools for Monthly AI Stack Recommendations

Every time a new AI tool drops, I ask three simple questions before I even think about adding it to my stack.

**Does it save me real time?** Not five minutes here and there. I mean does it cut hours out of my week? If a tool shaves off ten minutes but takes thirty to set up, that's a bad trade.

**Can I learn it in a day?** If I need a weekend course just to get started, it's probably built for developers. The best tools for non-developers feel obvious within an hour.

**Does it replace something more expensive?** Maybe it replaces a freelancer. Maybe it kills another subscription. If it doesn't make something cheaper, it's just adding cost.

These three questions are the backbone of my monthly AI stack recommendations. They keep me honest.

> **Tip:** Before subscribing to any new AI tool, use this quick prompt to pressure-test whether you actually need it:

```
I'm considering adding [Tool Name] to my AI stack. Here's what I currently use:
- Thinking: [your chat tool]
- Building: [your builder tool]
- Connecting: [your automation tool]

I want to use [Tool Name] for: [describe what you'd use it for]

Does this overlap with what my current tools already do? What specific task would this tool handle that my current stack cannot? Be honest and direct.
```

Here's the other thing I've learned — demos lie. A tool can look incredible in a two-minute video and then fall apart when you try to use it for your actual project. I only recommend tools I've used on real work, not ones that impressed me in a tweet.

Your feedback matters too. When readers tell me a tool worked great or flopped for them, that shapes next month's picks. Real results from real people beat any marketing page.

## The Three Layers Every Non-Developer AI Stack Needs

Think of your AI stack like a sandwich. It needs three layers to hold together. Skip one and the whole thing falls apart.

**Layer 1: Thinking.** This is your AI chat tool — the place where you brainstorm, ask questions, draft content, and reason through problems. ChatGPT or Claude lives here. You only need one.

**Layer 2: Building.** This is where ideas become real things. Tools like Replit or Cursor let you create apps, websites, and dashboards without writing traditional code. Again, one tool is enough.

**Layer 3: Connecting.** This is the automation layer. It links your tools together so things happen without you. Think: a form submission automatically adds a row to a spreadsheet and sends an email. Make or Zapier handles this.

> **Warning:** The most common stack mistake I see is loading up on Layer 1 (thinking) tools — subscribing to ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini all at once — while completely ignoring Layer 3 (connecting). If you're manually copying data between apps, you're leaving the biggest time savings on the table. One automation tool will save you more hours than three chat tools combined.

Here is the key insight behind my monthly AI stack recommendations: **you need one tool per layer, not five.** When people feel stuck, it is usually because they are missing a layer entirely — not because they need another subscription.

Most non-developers load up on thinking tools but skip connecting. That means they are doing repetitive work by hand that a simple automation could handle in seconds. ## Common Mistakes When Choosing Your AI Stack

Let me save you some headaches. I've made all of these mistakes myself.

**Signing up for every new tool.** A shiny new AI app launches on Tuesday. By Wednesday, you've got another $20/month subscription. By the end of the month, you're paying for six tools that mostly do the same thing. This is subscription creep, and it's the number one reason I started publishing monthly AI stack recommendations. You need a system for saying no.

**Picking tools built for developers.** Some AI tools are incredibly powerful — if you know how to code. If you don't, you'll spend three hours stuck on setup and feel like you're the problem. You're not. The tool just wasn't made for you. Always check: can I actually use this today, without Googling what a terminal is?

**Skipping automation.** This one stings because it's invisible. People grab a chat tool and a builder tool, then move data between them by hand. Copy, paste, repeat. Automation is the layer that connects everything. It saves more time than any other piece of your stack, and most people ignore it completely.

> **Tip:** Do a monthly "stack audit" on the 1st of every month. Open your subscription list and ask yourself three questions about each tool: Did I use this in the last 7 days? Could another tool I already pay for do the same thing? Would I re-subscribe if I had to start from scratch today? If the answer to all three is no, cancel it. You can always re-subscribe later.

One good tool per layer beats five overlapping tools every single time. If you're not sure how many tools you actually need, my post on the [minimum AI tools stack for beginners](https://derekjensen.io/blog/minimum-ai-tools-stack-for-beginners-just-3-tools) breaks it down to just three.

## FAQ

### What is the best AI stack?

There is no single best stack for everyone. It depends on what you are building and what problems you are solving. But for most non-developers, a solid baseline is one AI chat tool, one app builder, and one automation tool. That covers thinking, building, and connecting — the three layers I talked about above. If you want to see the specific tools I recommend right now, check out my current monthly AI stack recommendations in the June 2026 section of this post. I update these every month because what works best keeps changing.

### What is the 10-20-70 rule for AI?

This is a simple way to think about where AI value actually comes from. About 10% comes from the AI model itself. Another 20% comes from the data you feed it. And a full 70% comes from how you integrate and use it in your daily work. This is great news for non-developers. You do not need the fanciest model. You need to get good at using the tools you already have. That is exactly why picking the right stack — and learning it well — matters more than chasing every new release.

### Which AI is best for full stack development?

If you are not an engineer, "full stack" might sound intimidating. But it really just means handling both the front-end (what people see) and the back-end (the logic behind it). Today, you can combine a builder like Replit or Cursor with an automation tool to cover the full picture. No traditional coding knowledge needed. You are still doing full stack development — you are just using AI tools instead of writing code from scratch. If you want to understand the difference between front-end and back-end in plain language, my post on [frontend vs backend explained simply](https://derekjensen.io/blog/frontend-vs-backend-explained-simply-no-code-needed) breaks it down.
